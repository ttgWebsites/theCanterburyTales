Themes

  • Courtly love
  • Throughout Chaucer's tales the idea of courtly love is very prevalent
  • Courtly love refers to a medieval ideal that for some, particularly in non married couples, love was very overwhelming, powerful, and even erotic
  • Some for example were so overcome by love they could not eat, sleep, or live without their partner
  • This ideal was not so much occurring during the middle ages, however it was very often discussed in literature
  • Their are two specific examples in the Canterbury tales, the Squire in the General Prologue, and the Prioress
  • The Squire's example of courtly love is scene throughout the prologue, and it effects the way he dresses, acts, and tells his stories
  • The Prioress has a brooch that reads "love conquers all" another distinct example of the idea of courtly love
  • The corrupt church
  • During the time period The Canterbury Tales was written, the Catholic church was widely criticized
  • This was a time period of great famine and suffering for many people in Europe, yet the Catholic Church was very wealthy
  • The Church was building enormous cathedrals decorated ornately with lots of gold, which seemed to an extent absolutely ridiculous
  • Their were many stories of corrupt church members who took bribes, and were extremely well off and didn't lack the decency to look twice at the poor and suffering
  • Chaucer mocks the church in his writing with his characters
  • Many of his characters affiliated with the church are shown to stray from the normal churchly duties
  • They seem to prefer the life of the aristocrat rather than the devotional life
  • The narrator seems to harbor a grudge towards ecclesiastical officials, and often describes them in poor light
  • The importance of company
  • Throughout the tales Chaucer emphasizes the importance of company
  • Specifically a group of socially close people. During the medieval time period, a community's success depended on the social groups of workers and how close they people were connected
  • These companies often ate together, and had meeting halls that allowed them to stay in constant contact with one another
  • Chaucer uses company a lot during his tales, as often at the end of the tales, the person telling it would refer to the company of people and wish them well
  • Companies allowed a level playing field for the lower class, as it allowed them to come together and therefore took away some of the power of the noble authority
